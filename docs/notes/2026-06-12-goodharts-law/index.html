Goodhart's Law: When a measure becomes a target

Goodhart's Law states that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure because people start gaming the system to hit the number.

Takeaway

Focus on the underlying goal rather than optimizing for a single metric that can be gamed.

What I learned

Goodhart's Law is named after economist Charles Goodhart. It suggests that once a specific statistical measure is used for control or policy-making, it loses its ability to reliably represent the system. This happens because individuals and organizations change their behavior to optimize for that specific number, often at the expense of the original objective.

Why it matters

In software engineering and management, we rely heavily on metrics (KPIs) to track progress. However, if you incentivize a team based solely on "Lines of Code," they will write verbose, redundant code. If you reward "Number of Bugs Fixed," they might intentionally introduce small bugs just to fix them. The metric stops being a signal of quality and becomes a target for manipulation.

A simple example

The "Cobra Effect" is a famous instance of this. During British rule in India, the government offered a bounty for dead cobras to reduce the population. In response, people started breeding cobras to kill them and collect the bounty. When the government realized this and ended the program, the breeders released the cobras, and the population ended up higher than when they started.

How I can use it

  • Use a "Basket" of Metrics: Never rely on a single number. Use multiple, sometimes conflicting metrics (e.g., Velocity vs. Quality) to provide a balanced view.
  • Monitor Proxies: Treat metrics as proxies for reality, not reality itself. If the metric is improving but the "feel" of the project is getting worse, the metric is likely being gamed.
  • Contextualize Data: Always pair quantitative data with qualitative feedback to see if the target is being hit through genuine improvement or through shortcuts.
  • Review Incentives: Periodically ask: "How could someone achieve this goal while actually making the system worse?"
