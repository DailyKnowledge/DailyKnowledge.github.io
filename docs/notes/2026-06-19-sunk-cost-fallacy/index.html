Sunk Cost Fallacy: Don't throw good money after bad

The Sunk Cost Fallacy is the tendency to continue a behavior or endeavor as a result of previously invested resources (time, money, or effort), even when the current costs outweigh the benefits.

Takeaway

Decisions should be based on future potential, not past investment.

What I learned

The Sunk Cost Fallacy occurs when we find it difficult to walk away from a project, relationship, or investment because we’ve already "put so much into it." However, those resources (the "sunk costs") are gone forever regardless of what you do next. Rational decision-making should only consider future costs and future benefits.

Why it matters

This fallacy keeps us stuck in situations that are no longer serving us. In business, it causes companies to pour millions into failing product lines. In life, it keeps us in dead-end jobs or unhappy relationships simply because we've already spent years there. We fear that stopping means "wasting" the past, but continuing only ensures the "waste" continues into the future.

A simple example

Imagine you buy a non-refundable ticket to a movie. Thirty minutes in, you realize the film is terrible and you aren't enjoying it. If you stay to "get your money's worth," you are losing both your money and two more hours of your life. The money is already gone; staying doesn't bring it back.

How I can use it

  • The "Clean Slate" Test: Ask yourself, "If I hadn't already invested in this, would I start it today?" If the answer is no, you are likely falling for the sunk cost fallacy.
  • Focus on Opportunity Cost: Every moment or dollar spent on a failing endeavor is a moment or dollar not spent on something that could actually succeed.
  • Set "Exit Conditions" Early: Before starting a project, define the conditions under which you will stop. It's easier to follow a plan than to make an emotional decision later.
  • Accept the Loss: Acknowledging that past resources are lost is not failure—it's the first step to making a better decision.
