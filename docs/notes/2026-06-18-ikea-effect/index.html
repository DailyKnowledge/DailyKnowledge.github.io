The IKEA Effect: The price of ownership

The IKEA Effect is a cognitive bias in which consumers place a disproportionately high value on products they partially created or assembled themselves.

Takeaway

We overvalue our own creations; seek objective feedback to avoid falling in love with your own flawed work.

What I learned

The IKEA Effect, named by researchers Michael Norton, Daniel Mochon, and Dan Ariely in 2011, describes our tendency to overvalue things we have put effort into. Whether it’s furniture, a line of code, or a business plan, the act of "labor" creates a psychological link that inflates the perceived value of the object, often far beyond its actual market worth or quality.

Why it matters

This bias can lead to "Not Invented Here" syndrome in organizations, where teams reject superior solutions from outside because they didn't build them. For individuals, it can lead to overconfidence in projects that should be pivoted or abandoned. We become blind to the flaws in our work because the effort we spent "assembling" it feels like part of the value.

A simple example

Imagine two people looking at the same coffee table. One bought it pre-assembled; the other spent four hours struggling with an Allen wrench to build it. If you ask them both to sell it, the person who built it will almost always ask for a higher price. They aren't just selling a table; they are subconsciously trying to recoup the value of their labor and the emotional pride of completion.

How I can use it

  • The "External Review" Rule: Always have someone who wasn't involved in the creation process evaluate your work.
  • Kill Your Darlings: Be willing to discard ideas or features you worked hard on if they no longer serve the goal.
  • Leverage for Engagement: If you want others to value a project, involve them in the "assembly" early on. Shared labor leads to shared ownership.
  • Distinguish Effort from Value: Remind yourself that "hard work" does not automatically equal a "good result."
