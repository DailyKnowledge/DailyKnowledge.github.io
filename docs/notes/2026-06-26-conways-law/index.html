Conway's Law: Systems reflect organizations

Conway's Law states that organizations design systems (especially software architectures) that copy the communication structures of those organizations.

Takeaway

To design a clean, modular system, first design a clean, modular organization; structure dictates architecture.

What I learned

Conway's Law, formulated by computer programmer Melvin Conway in 1967, states that "organizations which design systems are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations."

If an organization is split into three separate teams that rarely communicate directly, they will inevitably design a software architecture composed of three separate modules that interface only in ways that mirror the teams' relationships. The structure of the product is bounded by the social and political structure of the creators.

This law is not a recommendation, but an observation of human social behavior: software code is written by people, and interfaces are defined through conversations. When communication is difficult between groups, the technical boundaries between their software components will reflect those friction points.

Why it matters

Conway’s Law means that you cannot fix a messy system architecture simply by changing the code—you must change the organization. If you try to build a microservices architecture while keeping a highly centralized, single database administration team, you will end up building a distributed monolith because the database team will act as a central coordination bottleneck.

To counter this, systems thinkers use the "Inverse Conway Maneuver"—structuring teams to match the desired technical architecture. If you want a modular system, first organize your teams into small, independent, cross-functional units.

A simple example

Imagine a software company with two engineering teams: one located in New York and one located in London. Because of the time zone difference, they rarely speak in real-time. If they are tasked with building a web application, they will almost certainly split it into two main components (such as a separate frontend and backend, or two independent services) with a clean, heavily documented API between them. They build this interface not because it's technically optimal, but because it matches the way they communicate.

How I can use it

  • Design Teams First: Before writing code or deciding on an architecture, define your team boundaries. Align team scopes with your target software components.
  • Minimize Team Dependencies: Give teams full ownership over their modules (including frontend, backend, and testing) so they can ship updates without constantly coordinating with other teams.
  • Use the Inverse Conway Maneuver: If your software architecture is cluttered, restructure your company's communication channels and team boundaries to drive the code toward the desired modular state.
  • Identify Architecture Problems Early: If two technical modules are constantly experiencing integration issues, check if the two teams responsible for them are having political or communication issues.
