Dunbar's Number: The limit of friendship

Dunbar's Number is a suggested cognitive limit to the number of people with whom one can maintain stable social relationships—relationships where you know who they are and how they relate to others.

Takeaway

Our brains have a biological limit of about 150 social connections; design organizations, networks, and communities keeping this limit in mind.

What I learned

Dunbar's Number is a theory proposed by British anthropologist Robin Dunbar in the 1990s. By analyzing the correlation between primate brain size (specifically the neocortex) and social group size, Dunbar extrapolated that humans have a cognitive limit of approximately 150 stable social relationships.

These "stable relationships" are defined as those where you know who each person is, how they relate to you, and how they relate to every other person in the group. If you saw them at a bar or airport, you wouldn't feel awkward sitting down with them for a drink. Within this 150-limit group, there are distinct concentric circles: * 5 people: The support group (innermost core/family/best friends). * 15 people: The sympathy group (people you can lean on in a crisis). * 50 people: Close friends (regular contact). * 150 people: The Dunbar limit (stable social relationships). * 500 people: Acquaintances (known, but lack stable connection).

Why it matters

Understanding Dunbar's Number is vital for community design, organization structure, and software design. When groups expand past 150 people, trust and social cohesion begin to break down. They require formal rules, hierarchies, and bureaucracies to coordinate instead of relying on informal social bonds. It explains why successful tribal societies, military companies, and early-stage companies often split or restructure when their size exceeds 150.

A simple example

Historically, hunter-gatherer societies typically split into two separate bands when their population approached 150 because informal consensus-based decision-making became impossible. In modern business, companies like Gore-Tex (W. L. Gore & Associates) famously design their factories to hold a maximum of 150 employees. When a factory grows larger than 150, they build an entirely new factory next door. By keeping factories under Dunbar's limit, they maintain a flat hierarchy, high trust, and a culture of personal accountability.

How I can use it

  • Design High-Trust Teams: If a division or team grows beyond 150, proactively split it or introduce formal communication and alignment structures.
  • Audit Your Social Circle: Realize that your cognitive energy is finite. If you are trying to maintain deep relationships with hundreds of people online, you are likely spreading your attention too thin at the expense of your core support group.
  • Build Communities with Intention: When building communities or forums, recognize that once membership crosses 150, you must transition from a social peer-to-peer trust model to a moderated, rule-based system.
  • Respect the Layers: Prioritize your inner layers (the 5 and 15) to maintain strong mental health and deep emotional resilience.
