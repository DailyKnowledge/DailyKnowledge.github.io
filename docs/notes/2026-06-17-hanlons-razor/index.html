Hanlon's Razor: Never attribute to malice

Hanlon's Razor is a mental model that suggests we should "never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity" (or incompetence/ignorance).

Takeaway

Assume positive intent (or neutral incompetence) to reduce personal stress and build better relationships.

What I learned

Hanlon's Razor is an adage that provides a simple rule for navigating human interactions and errors. It suggests that when someone does something that negatively affects you, your first instinct should not be to assume they did it on purpose to hurt you. Instead, it’s much more likely that they were tired, distracted, misunderstood the situation, or simply made a mistake.

Why it matters

Assuming malice creates a "fight or flight" response, leading to defensiveness, anger, and damaged relationships. It creates a narrative where you are a victim and the other person is a villain. By applying Hanlon's Razor, you shift the narrative to a "problem-solving" mode. It allows you to maintain emotional stability and respond rationally rather than emotionally.

A simple example

If a colleague misses a deadline for a project you are working on, you might think: "They are trying to sabotage my promotion!" (Malice). However, applying the Razor suggests: "Maybe they were overwhelmed with other work or forgot to set a reminder" (Incompetence/Ignorance). The latter allows you to offer help or send a friendly reminder, whereas the former leads to a confrontation.

How I can use it

  • Pause Before Reacting: When you feel slighted, take a breath and ask: "Is there a non-malicious explanation for this?"
  • Ask for Clarification: Instead of accusing, ask: "I noticed X happened; can you help me understand the context?"
  • Practice Empathy: Remember that everyone is fighting their own battles and has their own distractions.
  • Save the Malice Theory for Later: Only assume malice if you have clear, overwhelming evidence that cannot be explained any other way.
