Confirmation bias makes evidence feel more convincing when it agrees with me

People tend to notice, remember, and trust information that supports what they already believe.

Takeaway

Before deciding, look for evidence that could change my mind, not only evidence that feels familiar.

What I learned

Confirmation bias is the tendency to favor information that supports an existing belief. It can show up in what I notice, what I remember, and what I choose to trust.

Why it matters

If I only gather agreeing evidence, my confidence can grow without becoming more accurate. The danger is not just being wrong; it is becoming more certain for the wrong reasons.

A simple example

If I think a project is failing, I may pay attention to every small setback and ignore signs of progress. If I think it is succeeding, I may do the opposite.

How I can use it

  • Look for disconfirming evidence on purpose.
  • Ask what would change my mind.
  • Separate familiarity from truth.
  • Notice when I am curating evidence instead of testing it.
