Regression to the mean makes extreme results look temporary

When a result is unusually high or low, the next measurement often moves closer to the average.

Takeaway

Do not over-explain one extreme result before checking whether ordinary variation could pull it back.

What I learned

Regression to the mean is the tendency for unusually extreme measurements to be followed by measurements that are closer to the average. This happens when a result is partly shaped by random variation.

Why it matters

Extreme outcomes are tempting to explain with a dramatic story. A student has one terrible test, a team has one amazing month, or a metric suddenly spikes. Some of that change may be real, but some may simply be noise.

A simple example

If a basketball player has an unusually great shooting night, the next game is more likely to look ordinary. That does not mean the player got worse. It may mean the first night included both skill and unusually favorable variation.

How I can use it

  • Compare results against a longer baseline.
  • Be careful when judging people or systems after one extreme event.
  • Look for repeated evidence before assuming a new trend.
  • Ask how much randomness might be inside the measurement.
