Simpson's Paradox: The danger of aggregated data

Simpson's Paradox is a statistical phenomenon where a trend appears in several different groups of data but disappears or reverses when these groups are combined.

Takeaway

Aggregated data can be highly misleading; always investigate the underlying subgroups before drawing conclusions.

What I learned

Simpson's Paradox (or the Yule-Simpson effect) occurs when a correlation observed in different groups is reversed when the groups are combined. This happens due to "lurking variables"—hidden factors that differ between the groups and influence the overall outcome. When analyzing data, simply looking at the aggregate numbers without slicing them by relevant categories can lead to completely wrong decisions.

Why it matters

This model explains why data analysis must be rigorous. In medicine, a drug might appear more effective than another overall, but when patients are split by severity (mild vs. severe), the other drug is actually better for both groups. In business and product development, aggregate metrics (like conversion rate) might show a downward trend, while every individual user segment (e.g. mobile, desktop) is actually improving.

A simple example

Imagine a university admissions department. Men apply to easy departments in large numbers, while women apply to highly competitive departments with low acceptance rates. Even if every single department accepts a higher percentage of female applicants than male applicants (showing bias in favor of women within departments), the aggregate acceptance rate might show a higher percentage of men accepted overall (showing bias in favor of men at the university level) simply because more men applied to high-acceptance rate fields.

How I can use it

  • Segment Your Data: Never rely solely on high-level averages or aggregates. Drill down into cohorts, demographics, or user types.
  • Identify Lurking Variables: Ask, "What hidden factors could be driving this distribution?" (e.g. sample size, user intent, severity).
  • Design Clean Experiments: Use randomized controlled trials where groups are stratified to ensure key variables are balanced across cohorts.
  • Be Skeptical of Simple Headlines: When reading statistics in the news, look for the underlying breakdown of groups.
