The Lindy Effect: Why old ideas last longer

The Lindy Effect is the idea that the future life expectancy of non-perishable things, like ideas or books, is proportional to their current age.

Takeaway

If an idea has lasted for 50 years, it is likely to last another 50; if it has lasted 2,000 years, it will likely last another 2,000.

What I learned

The Lindy Effect suggests that for non-perishable things (ideas, books, languages, technologies), time acts as a filter rather than a cause of decay. Unlike biological organisms that have a "shelf life" (where being older means being closer to death), non-perishable items become more likely to survive the longer they have already existed. This concept was popularized by Nassim Taleb in his book Antifragile.

Why it matters

In an era of information overload and "the new thing," we often fall victim to neomania—the obsession with the newest tools and ideas. The Lindy Effect reminds us that the most robust knowledge is that which has already survived the test of time. New ideas are fragile; they haven't faced the "cruelty" of time yet. The classics are antifragile; they have survived wars, regime changes, and cultural shifts.

A simple example

Consider the Iliad by Homer. It has survived for roughly 2,800 years. According to the Lindy Effect, we can expect it to be read for another 2,800 years. In contrast, a best-selling business book published last month has no track record. Statistically, it is much more likely to be forgotten in a year than the Iliad is to disappear in a century.

How I can use it

  • Curate your reading: Spend more time reading "Lindy" books (classics) than chasing the latest trends. If a book is still in print after 50 years, it likely contains deeper truths than a new release.
  • Evaluate technologies: When choosing a library or framework for a long-term project, look at its age. A 10-year-old stable tool is often a safer bet than a 6-month-old "hyped" framework.
  • Investment in Skills: Focus on learning foundational skills (logic, mathematics, clear writing, human psychology) rather than specific software versions that will be obsolete in three years.
  • Respect Tradition: Recognize that social traditions that have existed for centuries often solve hidden problems that we don't fully understand yet.
