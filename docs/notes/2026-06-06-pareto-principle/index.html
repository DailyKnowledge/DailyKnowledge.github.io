Pareto Principle: Most results come from a few causes

The Pareto Principle states that roughly 80% of consequences come from 20% of causes.

Takeaway

Identify the small number of high-impact tasks and focus on them first to get the best results.

What I learned

The Pareto Principle, also known as the 80/20 rule, suggests that in many systems, about 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes. It was first observed by economist Vilfredo Pareto, who noticed that 80% of the land in Italy was owned by 20% of the population.

Why it matters

Understanding this principle helps in resource allocation. In a world of infinite options and limited time, it is crucial to recognize which inputs are truly productive. It prevents me from spending too much energy on the "trivial many" and forces me to focus on the "vital few."

A simple example

In software engineering, 80% of the bugs are often found in 20% of the code. Similarly, 80% of a company's revenue might come from 20% of its customers, or 80% of my productivity might come from 20% of my daily tasks.

How I can use it

  • Audit my to-do list: Which two items will produce 80% of the value?
  • Analyze failures: Which recurring problems cause most of the delays?
  • Optimize learning: Which core concepts explain 80% of the subject?
  • Say no more often: Filter out the low-impact 80% to protect time for the high-impact 20%.
