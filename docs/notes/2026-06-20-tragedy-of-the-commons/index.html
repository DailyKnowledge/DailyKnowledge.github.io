Tragedy of the Commons: The cost of shared resources

The Tragedy of the Commons is a situation in which individual users, who have open access to a shared resource, act independently according to their own self-interest and, contrary to the common good, deplete or spoil that resource.

Takeaway

Unregulated shared resources are always overexploited; we need governance, property rights, or mutual agreements to sustain them.

What I learned

The Tragedy of the Commons describes how individuals acting in their own self-interest can collectively destroy a shared resource, even when they all know it is vital to their long-term survival. The concept, popularized by ecologist Garrett Hardin in 1968, applies to resources that are "rivalrous" (one person's consumption limits another's) and "non-excludable" (hard to prevent people from using it). Because the benefit of exploiting the resource goes directly to the individual, while the cost of depletion is shared among everyone, individual rational choices lead to collective ruin.

Why it matters

This model explains some of humanity's most difficult and critical challenges: overfishing in global oceans, air and water pollution, climate change, traffic congestion, and deforestation. It also manifests in everyday life: the messy office kitchen, a shared printer that is always out of ink, or a software codebase that accumulates "technical debt" because developers add features without maintaining the shared infrastructure.

A simple example

Imagine a shared pasture where local herders graze their cows. If a herder adds one extra cow, they receive 100% of the financial benefit of selling that cow. However, the cost of overgrazing the pasture is shared equally among all herders. Because the personal gain outweighs the personal cost, it is rational for the herder to add the cow. But since every herder reaches the same rational conclusion, they all add extra cows. Eventually, the pasture is ruined, and all cows starve.

How I can use it

  • Establish Governance and Rules: Put clear rules, quotas, or restrictions in place to regulate how a shared resource is used.
  • Internalize the Costs: Impose taxes, tolls, or fees (like carbon taxes or congestion pricing) so users pay for the negative impact they have on the resource.
  • Define Boundaries and Ownership: Divide the shared resource into private property or assign clear stewards to manage and protect specific parts.
  • Combat Technical Debt: In team projects, assign ownership of shared modules or allocate dedicated time for refactoring to ensure the health of the "commons."
