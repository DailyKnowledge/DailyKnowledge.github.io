Resonance happens when timing matches a system's natural rhythm

A small repeated push can create a large response when it arrives at the right frequency.

Takeaway

The size of an effect can depend less on force and more on timing.

What I learned

Resonance happens when a system is driven near its natural frequency. Instead of each push fading away, the pushes add together and the motion can grow much larger.

Why it matters

This explains why a child on a swing can go higher with small well-timed pushes. It also matters in bridges, buildings, musical instruments, electronics, and anything that vibrates.

A simple example

Pushing a swing randomly does not help much. Pushing at the same point in each cycle adds energy in the direction the swing already wants to move. The timing turns small inputs into a larger motion.

How I can use it

  • Look for the natural rhythm of a system.
  • Remember that repeated small inputs can accumulate.
  • Ask whether timing is amplifying or damping the result.
  • Be careful when a system starts responding more strongly than the input seems to justify.
