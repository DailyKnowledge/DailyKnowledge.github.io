Parkinson's Law of Triviality: Why we focus on the small things

Parkinson's Law of Triviality (also known as bike-shedding) is the observation that members of an organization give disproportionate weight to trivial issues because they are easier to understand than complex ones.

Takeaway

Trivial issues are discussed endlessly because anyone can understand them; keep meetings focused on high-impact, complex decisions first.

What I learned

Parkinson's Law of Triviality, formulated by British writer and historian C. Northcote Parkinson in 1957, states that the amount of attention a system or committee gives to any budget or agenda item is in inverse proportion to its actual cost or complexity.

Parkinson illustrated this with a hypothetical committee tasked with approving two projects: 1. A Nuclear Power Plant (\$150,000,000): Approved in 10 minutes. The project is so complex that most committee members don't understand the engineering, physics, or safety regulations. Rather than admit their ignorance or spend years studying, they stay silent and quickly approve it. 2. A Bicycle Shed for Employees (\$3,500): Debated for 45 minutes. Because everyone understands what a bicycle shed is, what materials to use, and what color to paint it, every member has an opinion and wants to show they are contributing.

This behavior, widely known in software engineering as "bike-shedding," describes our tendency to spend massive amounts of time and energy debating trivial, easy-to-understand details while neglecting complex, high-risk decisions.

Why it matters

Bike-shedding is a major driver of wasted time and cognitive fatigue in organizations. Meetings get hijacked by discussions about button padding, email formats, font selections, or the phrasing of a slide header, while critical architecture patterns, business models, or technical debt go unaddressed. It occurs because sharing an opinion on a simple topic feels safe and yields quick dopamine, whereas solving a complex problem requires hard, quiet, vulnerable thinking.

A simple example

Imagine a software team designing a new platform. The database schema design meeting is scheduled for 30 minutes, but it finishes in 10 minutes with little debate because database indexing and sharding are complex subjects that only one senior developer understands. Immediately following, the team spends the remaining 20 minutes (and schedules a follow-up meeting) debating whether the success alert message on the user interface should be green or teal. The alert color is trivial, but everyone feels qualified to weigh in.

How I can use it

  • Structure Agendas Strategically: Place complex, high-stakes decisions at the very beginning of meetings when energy is high. Set strict time limits for simple, low-risk items.
  • Appoint Clear Owners: Don't use consensus-based committees for trivial choices. Assign a single owner (e.g., "The designer chooses the button colors; we will not debate them in this meeting") to prevent collective bike-shedding.
  • Acknowledge the Cognitive Bias: If you catch yourself or your team debating a small detail for more than 5 minutes, explicitly state: "We are bike-shedding right now. Let's make a quick choice or let the owner decide offline, and move back to the core architecture."
  • Optimize for Reversibility: Ask, "Is this decision easily reversible?" If yes (like a paint color or wording choice), make it instantly without debate. If no (like a database schema or contract commitment), allocate the team's focus there.
