Parkinson's Law: Work expands to fill time

Parkinson's Law is the observation that "work expands so as to fill the time available for its completion.

Takeaway

To be truly productive, set tight but realistic deadlines and focus on the minimum viable output.

What I learned

Parkinson's Law was first articulated by Cyril Northcote Parkinson as the opening line of an essay in The Economist in 1955. It suggests that if you give yourself a week to complete a two-hour task, the task will psychologically increase in complexity and perceived importance until it occupies the entire week. This happens through procrastination, over-engineering, or focusing on trivial details.

Why it matters

Understanding this law is crucial for time management and organizational efficiency. It explains why large bureaucracies often become less efficient as they grow—the amount of work to be done expands to fill the time of the increased staff. On an individual level, it warns us that "busyness" is often a self-inflicted trap created by generous deadlines rather than the inherent difficulty of the work.

A simple example

If you have a month to write a report, you might spend the first two weeks "researching" (scrolling), the third week drafting complex outlines, and the final week in a frantic rush to finish. However, if you were told the report was due in 48 hours, you would likely cut through the noise, focus on the core message, and produce a high-quality result in a fraction of the time.

How I can use it

  • Set Artificial Deadlines: Give yourself less time than you think you need. Treat these deadlines as "hard" stops to force prioritization.
  • Focus on ROI: Identify the 20% of the work that produces 80% of the results (Pareto Principle) and do that first.
  • Limit Available Resources: Just as work expands to fill time, it also expands to fill budget and staff. Keep projects lean.
  • Batch Small Tasks: Group minor tasks together and give them a single, short time block to prevent them from bleeding into your whole day.
