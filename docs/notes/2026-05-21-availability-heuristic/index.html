The availability heuristic makes vivid examples feel common

Events that are easy to remember can feel more frequent or more likely than they really are.

Takeaway

Before judging how common something is, ask whether it is truly frequent or just easy to recall.

What I learned

The availability heuristic is a mental shortcut where the mind estimates likelihood by how easily examples come to memory. Recent, emotional, repeated, or vivid examples can feel more representative than they are.

Why it matters

This shortcut is useful because memory often does contain real signals. But it can also distort judgment. A dramatic story in the news, one painful mistake, or one memorable success can outweigh quieter base rates.

A simple example

After hearing several stories about flight accidents, flying may feel more dangerous even if the actual risk has not changed. The examples are available in memory, so the risk feels larger.

How I can use it

  • Separate memorable from common.
  • Look for base rates before trusting a vivid example.
  • Notice when recent events are shaping my estimate.
  • Ask what evidence I would see if the less memorable cases were counted too.
